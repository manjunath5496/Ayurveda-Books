<p><strong>Ayurveda</strong>&nbsp;(<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a title="Help:IPA/English" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English">/<span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ʊər/: 'our' in 'tour'">ʊər</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span>,<span class="wrap">&nbsp;</span>-<span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span>-/</a></span></span>)&nbsp;is a system of medicine with historical roots in the&nbsp;<a title="Indian subcontinent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>.&nbsp;Globalized and modernized practices derived from Ayurveda traditions are a type of&nbsp;<a title="Alternative medicine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_medicine">alternative medicine</a>.&nbsp;In countries beyond India, Ayurvedic therapies and practices have been integrated in general&nbsp;<a title="Wellness (alternative medicine)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wellness_(alternative_medicine)">wellness</a>&nbsp;applications and in some cases in medical use.</p>
<p>The main classical Ayurveda texts begin with accounts of the transmission of medical knowledge from the Gods to sages, and then to human physicians.&nbsp;In&nbsp;<em><a title="Sushruta Samhita" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sushruta_Samhita">Sushruta Samhita</a></em>&nbsp;(<em>Sushruta's Compendium</em>),&nbsp;<a title="Sushruta" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sushruta">Sushruta</a>&nbsp;wrote that&nbsp;<a title="Dhanvantari" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhanvantari">Dhanvantari</a>,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu">Hindu</a>&nbsp;god of Ayurveda, incarnated himself as a king of Varanasi and taught medicine to a group of physicians, including Sushruta.&nbsp;Ayurveda therapies have varied and evolved over more than two millennia.&nbsp;Therapies are typically based on complex herbal compounds, minerals and metal substances (perhaps under the influence of early Indian alchemy or&nbsp;<em><a title="Rasa shastra" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rasa_shastra">rasa shastra</a></em>). Ancient Ayurveda texts also taught surgical techniques, including&nbsp;<a title="Rhinoplasty" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhinoplasty">rhinoplasty</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Lithotomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithotomy">kidney stone extractions</a>, sutures, and the extraction of foreign objects.</p>
<p>Although laboratory experiments suggest it is possible that some substances used in Ayurveda might be developed into effective treatments, there is no scientific evidence that any are effective as currently practiced. Ayurveda medicine is&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudoscientific" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscientific">pseudoscientific</a>.&nbsp;Other researchers consider it a&nbsp;<a title="Protoscience" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoscience">protoscience</a>, or trans-science system instead.&nbsp;In a 2008 study, close to 21% of Ayurveda U.S. and Indian-manufactured patent medicines sold through the Internet were found to contain toxic levels of&nbsp;<a title="Heavy metals" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_metals">heavy metals</a>, specifically&nbsp;<a title="Lead" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead">lead</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Mercury (element)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element)">mercury</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Arsenic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic">arsenic</a>.&nbsp;The public health implications of such metallic contaminants in India are unknown.</p>
<p>Some scholars assert that Ayurveda originated in prehistoric times,&nbsp;and that some of the concepts of Ayurveda have existed from the time of the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley Civilization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization">Indus Valley Civilization</a>&nbsp;or even earlier.&nbsp;Ayurveda developed significantly during the&nbsp;<a title="Vedic period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedic_period">Vedic period</a>&nbsp;and later some of the non-Vedic systems such as Buddhism and Jainism also developed medical concepts and practices that appear in the classical Ayurveda texts.&nbsp;<em><a title="Dosha" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dosha">Doṣa</a></em>&nbsp;balance is emphasized, and suppressing natural urges is considered unhealthy and claimed to lead to illness.&nbsp;Ayurveda treatises describe three elemental&nbsp;<em>doṣas</em>&nbsp;viz.&nbsp;<em>vāta</em>,&nbsp;<em>pitta a</em>nd&nbsp;<em>kapha</em>, and state that equality (Skt.&nbsp;<em>sāmyatva</em>) of the&nbsp;<em>doṣas</em>&nbsp;results in health, while inequality (<em>viṣamatva</em>) results in disease. Ayurveda treatises divide medicine into eight canonical components. Ayurveda practitioners had developed various medicinal preparations and surgical procedures from at least the beginning of the&nbsp;<a title="Common Era" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era">common era</a>.</p>

<h2><span id="Eight_components" class="mw-headline">Eight components</span></h2>
<p>The earliest classical&nbsp;<a title="Sanskrit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>&nbsp;works on Ayurveda describe medicine as being divided into eight components (Skt.&nbsp;<em>aṅga</em>).&nbsp;This characterization of the physicians' art, "the medicine that has eight components" (Skt.&nbsp;<em>cikitsāyām aṣṭāṅgāyāṃ</em>&nbsp;चिकित्सायामष्टाङ्गायाम्), is first found in the Sanskrit epic the&nbsp;<a title="Mahabharata" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahabharata"><em>Mahābhārata</em></a>, c. 4th century BCE.&nbsp;The components are:</p>
<ul>
<li><em>Kāyachikitsā</em>: general medicine, medicine of the body</li>
<li><em>Kaumāra-bhṛtya</em>&nbsp;(Pediatrics): Discussions about prenatal and postnatal care of baby and mother, methods of conception; choosing the child's gender, intelligence, and constitution; and childhood diseases and midwifery.</li>
<li><em>Śalyatantra</em>:&nbsp;<a title="History of surgery" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_surgery#India">surgical techniques</a>&nbsp;and the extraction of foreign objects</li>
<li><em>Śhālākyatantra:</em>&nbsp;treatment of ailments affecting ears, eyes, nose, mouth, etc. ("ENT")</li>
<li><em>Bhūtavidyā</em>: pacification of possessing spirits, and the people whose minds are affected by such possession</li>
<li><em><a title="Agada" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agada">Agadatantra</a></em>/<em>Vishagara-vairodh Tantra</em>&nbsp;(Toxicology): It includes subjects about epidemics, toxins in animals, vegetables and minerals. It as well contain keys for recognizing those anomalies and their antidotes.</li>
<li><em>Rasāyantantra</em>:&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Rejuvenation (aging)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rejuvenation_(aging)">rejuvenation</a>&nbsp;and tonics for increasing lifespan, intellect and strength</li>
<li><em>Vājīkaraṇatantra</em>:&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Aphrodisiacs" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphrodisiacs">aphrodisiacs</a>&nbsp;and treatments for increasing the volume and viability of semen and sexual pleasure. It also deals with infertility problems (for those hoping to conceive) and spiritual development (transmutation of sexual energy into spiritual energy).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Principles_and_terminology" class="mw-headline">Principles and terminology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">&nbsp;</div>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<p>The word "ayurveda" is&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Sanskrit language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit_language">Sanskrit</a>:&nbsp;<span lang="sa"><a class="extiw" title="wikt:आयुर्वेद" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%86%E0%A4%AF%E0%A5%81%E0%A4%B0%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B5%E0%A5%87%E0%A4%A6">आयुर्वेद</a></span>,&nbsp;<em lang="sa-Latn" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration"><em>Āyurveda</em></em>, meaning knowledge of life and longevity.</p>
<p>The central theoretical ideas of Ayurveda developed in the mid-first millennium BCE, and show parallels with&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Sāṅkhya" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C4%81%E1%B9%85khya">Sāṅkhya</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Vaiśeṣika" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vai%C5%9Be%E1%B9%A3ika">Vaiśeṣika</a>&nbsp;philosophies, as well as with&nbsp;<a title="Buddhism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism">Buddhism</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Jainism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jainism">Jainism</a>.&nbsp;Balance is emphasized, and suppressing natural urges is considered unhealthy and claimed to lead to illness.&nbsp;For example, to suppress sneezing is said to potentially give rise to shoulder pain.&nbsp;However, people are also cautioned to stay within the limits of reasonable balance and measure when following nature's urges.&nbsp;For example, emphasis is placed on moderation of food intake,&nbsp;sleep, and sexual intercourse.</p>
<p>Ayurveda names seven basic tissues (<a class="mw-redirect" title="Dhatu (Ayurveda)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhatu_(Ayurveda)">dhatu</a>), which are plasma (<em>rasa</em>), blood (<em>rakta</em>), muscles (<em>māmsa</em>), fat (<em>meda</em>), bone (<em>asthi</em>),&nbsp;<a title="Bone marrow" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bone_marrow">marrow</a>&nbsp;(<em>majja</em>), and semen (<em>shukra</em>). Like the medicine of classical antiquity, Ayurveda has historically divided bodily substances into five&nbsp;<a title="Classical element" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element#Classical_elements_in_Hinduism">classical elements</a>, (Sanskrit)&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Panchamahabhuta" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panchamahabhuta">panchamahabhuta</a></em>, viz.&nbsp;<a title="Prithvi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prithvi">earth</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Ap (water)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ap_(water)">water</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Agni" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agni">fire</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Vayu" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vayu">air</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ether (classical element)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ether_(classical_element)">ether</a>.&nbsp;There are also twenty&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Guna" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guna">gunas</a>&nbsp;(qualities or characteristics) which are considered to be inherent in all matter. These are organized in ten pairs: heavy/light, cold/hot, unctuous/dry, dull/sharp, stable/mobile, soft/hard, non-slimy/slimy, smooth/coarse, minute/gross, and viscous/liquid.</p>
<p>Ama (a&nbsp;<a title="Sanskrit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>&nbsp;word meaning "uncooked" or "undigested" ) is used to refer to the concept of anything that exists in a state of incomplete transformation. With regards to&nbsp;<a title="Oral hygiene" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_hygiene">oral hygiene</a>, it is claimed to be a toxic byproduct generated by improper or incomplete&nbsp;<a title="Digestion" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digestion">digestion</a>.&nbsp;The concept has no equivalent in&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Standard medicine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_medicine">standard medicine</a>.</p>
<p>Ayurveda also names three elemental bodily humors, the&nbsp;<em>doshas</em>&nbsp;(called Vata, Pitta and Kapha), and states that a balance of the doshas results in health, while imbalance results in disease. One Ayurvedic view is that the doshas are balanced when they are equal to each other, while another view is that each human possesses a unique combination of the doshas which define this person's temperament and characteristics. In either case, it says that each person should modulate their behavior or environment to increase or decrease the doshas and maintain their natural state.</p>
<p>In medieval taxonomies of the Sanskrit knowledge systems, Ayurveda is assigned a place as a subsidiary&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Veda" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veda">Veda</a>&nbsp;(<a class="mw-redirect" title="Upaveda" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upaveda">upaveda</a>).&nbsp;Some medicinal plant names from the&nbsp;<em><a title="Atharvaveda" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atharvaveda">Atharvaveda</a></em>&nbsp;and other Vedas can be found in subsequent Ayurveda literature.&nbsp;The earliest recorded theoretical statements about the canonical models of disease in Ayurveda occur in the earliest&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist Canon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_Canon">Buddhist Canon</a>.</p>
<h2><span id="Practice" class="mw-headline">Practice</span></h2>
<p>Ayurvedic doctors regard physical existence, mental existence, and personality as their own units, with each element being able to influence the others.&nbsp;This is a&nbsp;<a title="Holism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holism#Medicine">holistic</a>&nbsp;approach used during diagnosis and therapy, and is a fundamental aspect of Ayurveda. Another part of Ayurvedic treatment says that there are channels (<em>srotas</em>) which transport fluids, and that the channels can be opened up by massage treatment using oils and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Swedana" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedana">Swedana</a>&nbsp;(fomentation). Unhealthy, or blocked, channels are thought to cause disease.</p>
<h3><span id="Diagnosis" class="mw-headline">Diagnosis</span></h3>
<p>Ayurveda has eight ways to diagnose illness, called&nbsp;<a title="Nadi (yoga)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nadi_(yoga)">Nadi</a>&nbsp;(pulse), Mootra (urine),&nbsp;<a title="Malas (Ayurveda)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malas_(Ayurveda)">Mala</a>&nbsp;(stool), Jihva (tongue), Shabda (speech), Sparsha (touch), Druk (vision), and Aakruti (appearance). Ayurvedic practitioners approach diagnosis by using the five senses.&nbsp;For example, hearing is used to observe the condition of breathing and speech.&nbsp;The study of the lethal points or&nbsp;<em>marman marma</em>&nbsp;is of special importance.</p>
